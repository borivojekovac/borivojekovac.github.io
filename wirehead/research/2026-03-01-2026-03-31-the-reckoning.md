# The Reckoning: AI-Driven Layoffs, Brain Fry, and the K-Shaped Economy March Created

*March 2026 delivered the most concentrated evidence yet that AI is reshaping the labor market in real time. Forty thousand tech workers lost their jobs. A major study found AI tools are frying workers' brains. BlackRock's CEO warned of a K-shaped divergence. And the research data suggests we're only seeing the beginning.*

## The March Layoff Wave

The numbers are stark. In a single month:

- **Block (Square)**: 4,000 employees — **40% of workforce**. Jack Dorsey explicitly stated AI can automate much of the work these employees were doing.
- **Atlassian**: 1,600 employees — **10% of workforce**. Co-founder Mike Cannon-Brookes said "the bar for what 'great' looks like for software companies has gone up."
- **Dell**: 11,000 employees — **10% of workforce**. Second consecutive year of similar-scale cuts.
- **Meta**: Several hundred workers at month's end, with Reuters estimating 20% of workforce potentially impacted in coming waves.
- **Morgan Stanley**: 2,500 jobs across units — approximately 3% of workforce.

Reuters estimated that 60 Silicon Valley tech companies had laid off more than **38,000 employees** in the first quarter of 2026 alone.

What's different about the March wave isn't the scale — tech layoffs are not new. It's the **explicit causal attribution to AI**. Block's CEO didn't blame market conditions or "restructuring for efficiency." He said AI could do the work. Atlassian didn't frame it as cost-cutting. They framed it as rebalancing resources for "the AI era." Dell, cutting 10% for the second straight year, is visibly shrinking its human workforce while investing in AI infrastructure.

## The "AI Washing" Debate

Not everyone accepts the AI attribution at face value. Whoop CEO Will Ahmed, whose company is expanding its staff by 75%, told Bloomberg:

> "There's a lot of companies that are doing layoffs right now and blaming it on AI. But they're actually doing layoffs because the businesses aren't performing particularly well. And it's a convenient excuse."

The term **"AI washing"** — attributing layoffs to AI when the real drivers are financial performance or strategic misjudgment — gained traction in March as analysts questioned whether every company citing AI as the reason for cuts was being honest about the cause.

Microsoft, for instance, froze hiring in major groups including cloud (per The Information), but framed it as strategic resource allocation rather than AI replacement. The "low hire, low fire" pattern identified by ADP data — where companies are cautious about both hiring and firing — complicates the narrative that AI is causing a jobs apocalypse. ADP's private payroll data showed 62,000 new roles added in March, 40,000 more than expected.

The reality is likely both: AI is genuinely enabling some companies to operate with smaller workforces, while simultaneously providing cover for companies whose layoffs have more to do with poor business performance than technological displacement.

## Anthropic's Labor Market Study: The Data Behind the Anxiety

Anthropic released its workforce impact study in early March — and the findings landed with the force of a diagnosis nobody wanted to hear.

Using Claude's own usage data, Anthropic mapped which jobs are most exposed to AI automation:

- **75% of programming tasks** are already covered by AI usage
- **67% of data entry tasks** similarly automated
- **Customer service** follows closely

The demographic profile of the most vulnerable workers contradicts the popular assumption that blue-collar jobs are most at risk: the most exposed positions belong to **older, female, more educated, and higher-paid workers**. The Brookings Institution's parallel analysis found women hold 86% of the most vulnerable positions.

Goldman Sachs released a companion report the same month with broadly consistent findings, lending Wall Street credibility to the academic research.

What makes Anthropic's study particularly notable is that it comes from a company that directly profits from the displacement it's documenting. Anthropic's research team explicitly acknowledged the tension: the impact of AI "will be shaped not just by the capabilities as they advance, but also the choices that we make." Whether that framing is genuine responsibility or sophisticated reputation management is a question readers can decide for themselves.

## AI Brain Fry: The Cognitive Cost of Supervision

While the layoff headlines focused on workers being replaced by AI, a parallel story emerged about the workers who remain — and the psychological toll of working alongside AI systems.

The Boston Consulting Group published research in Harvard Business Review that coined the term **"AI brain fry"** — cognitive overload caused by excessive AI oversight. Their study of 1,488 US workers found:

- **14% of all AI users** experience brain fry (26% in marketing roles)
- Workers required to closely monitor AI outputs expend **14% more cognitive effort** than those doing equivalent non-AI tasks
- **33% more decision fatigue** among heavily supervised AI workflows
- Productivity **drops sharply** when using more than three AI tools simultaneously
- Workers described a **"buzzing" feeling** of sustained cognitive strain

The mechanism is insidious: as firms incentivize employees to supervise complex teams of agents — measuring and rewarding token consumption as a proxy for performance — people find themselves pushed to cognitive limits not by the work itself but by the effort of monitoring, correcting, and validating AI outputs.

BCG found that leadership-provided training and structured support significantly reduce brain fry, suggesting the problem is largely organizational, not inherent. But the finding that productivity drops when using more than three AI tools at once challenges the "more tools, more productivity" assumption underlying many enterprise AI strategies.

## Morgan Stanley's Power Warning

In early March, Morgan Stanley released its "Intelligence Factory" report with a warning that grabbed institutional attention: a **transformative AI leap is coming in H1 2026**, and most of the world isn't ready for it.

The report's most specific and alarming finding was infrastructural: Morgan Stanley's model projects a net **US power shortfall of 9 to 18 gigawatts through 2028** — a 12% to 25% deficit in the power needed to run AI infrastructure at the scale currently planned.

At Morgan Stanley's TMT Conference, the single most common investor question was about AI's impact on jobs — not the technology's capabilities, not the business opportunities, but the workforce displacement. Anxiety about what the technology means for the next generation of workers pervaded what should have been a room full of optimists.

The power constraint isn't abstract. If the US is 12-25% short of the power it needs for AI data centers, the constraint will shape which companies can scale, which regions can host data centers, and how fast the broader deployment of AI proceeds. It's a physical limit on a digital revolution.

## The K-Shaped Economy

BlackRock CEO Larry Fink's 2026 annual letter to shareholders, published March 23, provided the highest-profile framing of what all these trends mean collectively.

Fink argued that AI is creating **"K-shaped" results** — a divergence where leading firms surge while lagging firms struggle, with no middle ground. The same dynamic, he warned, applies to workers, regions, and entire economies.

The prescription was characteristically financial: Fink urged broader investment access — including Social Security reform to give more Americans exposure to capital markets — as the mechanism to prevent AI from widening the wealth gap to unsustainable levels.

The underlying observation is hard to dispute: the companies doing the laying off (Block, Atlassian, Dell) are not failing businesses. They're companies that have decided they can operate with fewer humans and more AI. The value that displaced workers created doesn't disappear — it shifts to the capital owners of the AI systems that replace them. Without mechanisms to distribute that value more broadly, the K-shape steepens.

## The Okta CEO's 40% Rule

Amid the layoff headlines and economic warnings, one data point from the enterprise world crystallized the pace of change. Okta CEO Todd McKinnon told Yahoo Finance that companies now need to **change approximately 40% of what they do each year** to avoid falling out of the competitive race.

That number deserves scrutiny. If a company needs to reinvent 40% of its operations annually just to stay competitive, the organizational strain is immense. It means perpetual restructuring, constant reskilling, and a workforce that can never achieve stability. Combined with BCG's finding that cognitive overload kicks in past three AI tools, and Anthropic's data showing which roles are most exposed, the picture is one of relentless, disorienting change imposed on the very workers asked to drive it.

## Hiring Amid the Cuts

The layoff narrative is incomplete without its counterweight. OpenAI is looking to **nearly double its headcount from 4,500 to 8,000** by end of 2026. Whoop is expanding by 75%. ADP data shows net job creation exceeding expectations.

The AI companies building the technology that enables layoffs elsewhere are themselves hiring aggressively. The labor market isn't simply shrinking — it's restructuring around a new set of skills where the premium goes to people who can orchestrate AI systems, exercise judgment on agent outputs, and navigate ambiguity that machines can't resolve.

As Notion co-founder Simon Last told Wired, the most prized human skill in the AI era is, ironically, **"agency"** — the ability to be "open-minded, curious, and willing to try whatever the newest thing is." The value of that kind of person, he argued, is "going up exponentially."

## What March Tells Us

March 2026 didn't create the AI-labor tension. It concentrated enough data points in one month to make the picture unmistakable:

1. **Major companies are explicitly attributing layoffs to AI capability.** Whether every attribution is honest is debatable; that the pattern is real is not.
2. **The workers most at risk are not who most people assume.** The most vulnerable are older, female, more educated, and higher-paid — exactly the demographic that thought their expertise made them safe.
3. **The workers who remain face a new kind of cognitive strain.** AI brain fry is a measurable phenomenon, and it gets worse as companies pile on more AI tools without adequate organizational support.
4. **Infrastructure constraints will shape the pace.** Morgan Stanley's power shortfall projection puts a physical ceiling on how fast AI deployment can scale, buying time but not changing the direction.
5. **The economic divergence is K-shaped and accelerating.** BlackRock's framing isn't alarmist — it's the consensus view from the world's largest asset manager.

The question is no longer whether AI will reshape the workforce. It's whether institutions — companies, governments, educational systems — can adapt fast enough to prevent the transition from creating more damage than value. March 2026 suggests the answer is not yet.
